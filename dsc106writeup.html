Design Rationale for Interactive COVID-19 Visualization

💡 Click on one state to see specific county data! Click again to zoom out! Hover on a state to see more information! 💡

Our visualization tool is aimed at presenting COVID-19 data at both state and county levels in an engaging and intuitive manner. We wanted to create a user-centric interface that facilitates an insightful exploration of complex data.

The choice of a blue color scale is selected for its natural appeal to the human eye and its effective representation of data gradation. To further enhance visual clarity, we settled on a discrete five-bin color scheme, striking an ideal balance that allows for clear differentiation without overwhelming the user. This consistency in color scaling across county and state data ensures effortless comparison, enabling a cohesive analytical experience.

Interactivity through zooming was integrated to manage the complexity of information presented. By revealing the county-level data only upon a closer inspection of a state, we avoid troubling the viewer with excessive detail. This approach keeps the initial view focused on state-level data, maintaining clarity and inviting deeper investigation on demand. Furthermore, the ability to zoom out with a simple click provides a streamlined navigation experience, quickly broadening the view to a national perspective without the need for manual zoom adjustments.

The legend plays a crucial role, acting as a reference through various levels of data exploration. It remains visible and constant, whether the user is analyzing the data at the state or county level, reinforcing the ease of comparative analysis.

The infobox feature is an example of our user-focused design, activated upon hovering over a state to provide supplemental information. This feature presents vital data such as state population and the percentage of the population with a positive COVID-19 diagnosis, complementing the broader data landscape outlined by the legend.

In our representation of data, we made a shift from using raw case numbers to a percentage-based metric scaled by population. This is because we found that raw case numbers disproportionately highlight areas with larger populations, thus skewing the true impact of the pandemic. By normalizing data against population size, we ensure a more accurate and insightful depiction of COVID-19's reach.

As for the county-level detail, we have designed the interface to zoom in on a state upon selection, revealing a detailed county map that adheres to the same color scale as the state data. This allows for a granular analysis of how the pandemic spreads across counties within a state, yet provides the option to 'zoom out' for a broader view, maintaining perspective on the overall trends.

During the development process, we assessed various alternatives. Initially, we considered displaying all county data from the outset but recognized the potential for information overload. A hover-based display for county data was also tested but ultimately rejected due to the small relative size of counties on the map, which made it difficult to view specific data effectively. Moreover, we contemplated relying solely on the legend for data representation but decided against it, recognizing the added value of the infobox in providing immediate, detailed data points. Lastly, the decision to move from raw case counts to population-scaled data was driven by our commitment to provide a more insightful analysis, unconfounded by population density.

Collectively, we engaged in coming up with ideas of the map. maintaining the map's interactivity, deliberating over color scales, and the coding process—particularly the zoom functions, which demanded careful attention to detail to ensure the accurate representation of colors, layering, and data synchronization.

In terms of time investment, our project unfolded in phases. We initially dedicated 2 hours to establishing our development environment and understanding the code organization. The following 2 hours were spent on data cleaning to prepare for the visualization. The most substantial time commitment, approximately 19 hours, was dedicated to the actual coding phase, where we brought the state and county maps to life with corresponding color-coded data. This phase challenged us the most, particularly in perfecting the zoom functionality, to guarantee a seamless and informative user experience.

We met the most hurdles when rendering the zoom functions. It was hard to make sure the correct information appeared when we zoomed in and all the distracting elements were removed from the screen. In addition, we spent a long time making sure our dataset matched up correctly with the topojson data. We first found that the counties may have duplicate names across different states and many counties had various possible names. This made it hard to map correct data to the map. We cleaned over 200 lines of specific county data with errors in them, and matched a FIPS data to each of the counties to make sure we have matched the counties with the topojson file correctly.

In conclusion, our project stands as a testament to the power of teamwork and the pursuit of creating a data visualization tool that is not only informative but also accessible and engaging for users from all walks of life.
